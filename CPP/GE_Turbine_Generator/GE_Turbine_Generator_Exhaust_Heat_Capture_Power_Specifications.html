GE TM2500 DLE — Base Platform

AERODERIVATIVE MOBILE / RAPID DEPLOY ~37 MW SIMPLE CYCLE DLE COMBUSTOR

The GE TM2500 DLE is a trailer-mounted aeroderivative gas turbine based on the LM2500 family, one of the most widely deployed gas turbines in the world. The DLE (Dry Low Emissions) combustor variant eliminates the need for water injection for NOₓ control, reducing operating complexity and water consumption entirely at the simple cycle stage.

The TM2500 DLE can scale output in ~37 MW blocks and is capable of reaching full power within 10 minutes, with installation and commissioning achievable in as few as 11 days — making it suitable for rapid-permanent deployment at industrial campuses.

Key insight: At ~37 MW electrical output, roughly 53–56 MWth rides out the exhaust stack. This thermal budget drives all downstream HRSG and steam turbine sizing decisions. The TM2500's aeroderivative lineage means higher pressure ratio but lower exhaust temperature (~900°F) compared to heavy-frame machines — a thermodynamic constraint that caps HRSG architecture.

02

Exhaust Heat Capture & Recovery

The TM2500 DLE is fully deployable with downstream heat recovery. A Heat Recovery Steam Generator (HRSG) connects at the exhaust flange, capturing waste thermal energy for power augmentation, process heat, or combined heat and power (CHP) applications. The TM2500's three-trailer mobility configuration is retained — the HRSG and emissions control equipment are pad-mounted permanent structures connected at the exhaust interface.

SCR catalyst placement: The selective catalytic reduction (SCR) bed is integrated into the HRSG ductwork at the optimal temperature window — typically 550–750°F for vanadium-based SCR. This positions the catalyst mid-HRSG, where exhaust has cooled to the active catalyst range, before the IP/LP evaporator sections.

Tri-Generation (CHPC) Configuration

For a data center campus, the HRSG steam loop can serve an absorption chiller system — converting recovered thermal energy directly into chilled water for cooling loads. This tri-generation (Combined Heat, Power, and Cooling) configuration extracts value from exhaust energy across three outputs simultaneously: electricity, process heat, and mechanical cooling.

03

Emissions Control Train

The TM2500 DLE combustor significantly reduces NOₓ formation upstream of the stack. A full post-combustion treatment train is required for Ohio EPA Title V or Synthetic Minor permit compliance, using a BACT (Best Available Control Technology) analysis. The DLE combustor + SCR stack is a well-established BACT pathway for this turbine class.

Cold-start window: SCR catalyst requires minimum exhaust temperatures (~550°F) to function. A brief emissions-non-compliant window exists on cold start (typically <3 minutes). Ohio EPA air permits must account for this via startup/shutdown exemption provisions — a standard provision in Title V permit packages for this turbine class.

04

Steam Turbine Bottoming Cycle

The TM2500 DLE combined cycle bottoming stage uses a steam turbine to convert HRSG-generated steam into additional electrical output. Three configurations are viable, each with distinct trade-offs between electrical output maximization and thermal energy delivery for campus use.

Configuration A — Backpressure Steam Turbine

Steam expands from HRSG inlet conditions to a usable process pressure (50–250 psig). The exhaust steam exits still hot and pressurized — directly usable as process heat, absorption chiller feed, or hot water supply. Electrical extraction is limited by the pressure drop across the turbine.

Configuration B — Condensing Steam Turbine

Steam expands through the full pressure range down to near-vacuum condenser pressure (~1–2 psia, ~100°F), extracting maximum enthalpy as shaft work and electricity. This is the maximum electrical output configuration. A real-world reference: the GE LM2500+G4 Gen04 Combined Cycle Power Plant achieves ~45 MW total output from a ~35 MW gas turbine base, with a 10–12 MW condensing steam turbine, delivering over 52% net plant efficiency.

Configuration C — Extraction-Condensing (Recommended)

The optimal configuration for a data center campus. Controlled extraction ports at intermediate pressure stages allow dynamic split between electrical generation and thermal delivery — dialing between full power mode and full CHP mode in real time based on campus load requirements.

Campus control logic: Peak IT load, low thermal demand → close extraction, maximize electrical output (~12 MW ST). Peak thermal demand (summer cooling or winter heating) → open extraction, trade ~2–4 MW electrical for 20+ MWth direct to the building loop. No equipment change required — purely a valve position adjustment.

Critical exhaust temperature difference: The 7HA.03's ~1,130°F+ exhaust versus the TM2500's ~900°F exhaust is not simply a performance delta — it is the thermodynamic threshold that determines which HRSG architecture is viable. The reheat cycle is generally limited to gas turbines with exhaust temperatures above 593°C (1,100°F). The 7HA.03 clears this threshold comfortably; the TM2500 does not, which is why their HRSG configurations differ structurally, not just in scale.

The world's first GE 7HA.03 gas turbines entered commercial operation at Florida Power & Light's Dania Beach Clean Energy Center on June 1, 2022. Two 7HA.03 units power a 1,260 MW combined cycle plant — the largest and most efficient 60 Hz combined cycle plant in operation at the time.

HRSG Architecture — Platform Differences

The heat recovery steam generator is the defining engineering difference between the two platforms. The TM2500 uses a compact single or dual pressure HRSG; the 7HA.03 requires a triple-pressure reheat HRSG — a fundamentally different system in size, cost, civil footprint, and operational integration.

TM2500 DLE: Single / Dual Pressure, No Reheat

The aeroderivative's lower exhaust temperature (~900°F) and modest mass flow makes a single or dual pressure unfired HRSG the appropriate design. Adding a third pressure level or reheat section yields diminishing returns — there is insufficient thermal head in the exhaust to support incremental tube surface cost. A supplemental duct burner can push toward dual pressure operation, but triple pressure reheat is uneconomical at this scale.

Strategic Summary — Cleveland Industrial Valley Applications

For a data center campus deployment at the Morabito site or Opportunity Corridor IPGD, the TM2500 DLE with extraction-condensing bottoming cycle represents the right machine for rapid-to-permanent power infrastructure: deployable in weeks, 74–100 MW per two-unit installation in combined cycle, fully permit-compliant with Ohio EPA BACT via SCR, and adaptable for absorption cooling via the thermal extraction loop.

The GE Vernova 7HA.03 is the correct specification for a utility-scale campus spine targeting 640 MW+ per unit in combined cycle — equivalent in scope to a major utility power plant project. The heat recovery package for the 7HA.03 is not a scaled-up version of the TM2500 system; it is a structurally different engineering category, requiring triple-pressure reheat HRSGs, a multi-casing GE STF-A650 class steam turbine producing 200–437 MW from the bottoming cycle alone, building-scale SCR infrastructure, and major civil foundations across 3–5 acres per unit. Both platforms share the same fundamental DLN combustor + SCR + CO catalyst emissions pathway — the chemistry is identical; the physical scale is categorically different.
