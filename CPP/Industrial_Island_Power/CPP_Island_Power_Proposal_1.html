The Problem

Data centers need a LOT of power — and can't get it fast enough.

Think about all the AI chatbots, streaming services, and apps you use every day. Every click and search happens inside a huge building called a data center — basically a giant warehouse full of computers that run 24/7. Those computers need enormous amounts of electricity, and right now, getting that electricity is a massive headache.

🚗

Here's an analogy that makes it click:

Imagine you want to open a restaurant, but the only way to get running water is to wait in a government line — and that line is 5 to 7 years long. You can't cook, you can't open, you just wait. That's exactly what data centers face when they try to connect to the electric grid in Ohio today.

⏳

Years of Waiting

Connecting to the big electric grid can take 5 to 7 years in Ohio. Companies like Amazon, Google, and Microsoft need power now — not in a decade.

📈

Exploding Demand

Ohio already ranks #5 in the nation for data centers. With AI booming, electricity demand from data centers could grow 40% or more by 2030.

🔌

Grid Can't Keep Up

AEP Ohio — the big electric company — has over 30,000 megawatts of data center requests it can't fulfill. They literally put a pause on new connections.

💸

Cleveland Losing Out

When data center companies can't get power fast, they build somewhere else. Cleveland loses jobs, tax revenue, and economic growth.

The Solution

What if data centers just… made their own power?

That's exactly what Cleveland Public Power's Island Power System Installation Program does. Instead of waiting years for the big grid, CPP helps data centers install their own small, clean power plants right on-site — ready in months, not years.

🏝️

Why "Island" Power?

An island doesn't depend on the mainland for its resources — it takes care of itself. An "Island Power" data center generates its own electricity completely independently. If the main power grid goes down, the data center keeps running. It's like having a giant, super-reliable generator that never needs to stop.

The Technology

Two powerful, clean generator technologies

These aren't the loud, smelly diesel generators you might picture. These are cutting-edge machines that burn fuel without a traditional flame — which means they're incredibly clean, quiet, and efficient.

⚡

Mainspring Linear Generator

Made by Mainspring Energy · California

Think of this like a very high-tech piston engine — except instead of powering a car, it generates electricity. Each unit produces 250 kilowatts (kW), and you can stack as many as you need. To hit 60 MW for the full program, you'd use about 240 of them. They've been commercially shipping since 2020 and have a proven track record.

✓ Commercially available right now
✓ Near-zero air pollution (99% cleaner than diesel)
✓ Powers up in under 10 seconds
✓ Works on natural gas, hydrogen, or biogas
✓ New factory opening near Pittsburgh in 2025
🔋

KARNO Power Module

Made by Hyliion · Texas

The KARNO uses a clever heating and cooling cycle — similar to how a refrigerator works, but in reverse — to turn heat into electricity. The big version comes in a standard shipping container (2 MW each), making it incredibly compact. Just 30 containers would power the entire 60 MW program. It can run on over 20 different fuels.

✓ Extremely compact — fits in a shipping container
✓ Works on 20+ different fuel types — even ammonia
✓ Very few moving parts = less maintenance
✓ 20% more efficient than conventional generators
✓ Large data centers already placing orders

Good for data centers. Great for Cleveland.

This program isn't just about electricity — it's about building Cleveland's economy, creating local jobs, and making our city a destination for the tech industry.

⚡

Power in months, not years

Skip the 5–7 year grid queue entirely. Island Power gets companies up and running in 3 to 6 months — before a competitor snags the opportunity.

The Plan

How we get to 60 MW — step by step.

This isn't a distant dream — it's a phased plan with real milestones, starting later this year.

📐
Phase 0 · Late 2025
Set It Up
CPP officially launches the program, signs agreements with Mainspring Energy and Hyliion, and selects the first 1–2 data center customers to pilot the program. City Council approves the new program rate structure.
🔌
Phase 1 · Early 2026
First Installation — 5 MW Pilot
Install the first 20-unit Mainspring generator array (5 MW of power) at a pilot data center in Cleveland. Run it for 90 days in full island mode, prove the concept works, and publish the results.
🚀
Phase 2 · 2026 – 2027
Full Program Launch — 20 MW
Open enrollment to all data centers in CPP's territory. Deploy both Mainspring and KARNO generators across multiple customer sites. CPP builds a dedicated Island Power support team to manage and monitor all installations.
⭐
Phase 3 · 2027 – 2028
Full 60 MW Goal + Hydrogen Transition
Reach the full 60 MW program target. Activate hydrogen fuel blending as the Port of Cleveland hydrogen hub comes online. Begin earning revenue by selling the combined power capacity into regional electricity markets — benefiting all CPP customers.
The Numbers

What does this cost — and who pays?

Here's the simple version: data centers pay for their own power systems. CPP makes money from running the program. Cleveland residents don't pay a penny extra.

🏗️
Cost per Megawatt
$2M–$2.8M
That's what it costs to install 1 MW of island power capacity — including generators, wiring, and controls. For 60 MW total, that's roughly $120–168M across all customers combined.
💵
CPP Annual Revenue
Up to $14M/yr
At full 60 MW enrollment, CPP earns about $14 million per year from program fees, financing, fuel management, and maintenance contracts — all paid by the data centers, not residents.
🏦
CPP Financing Rate
~4.5–5.5%
Because CPP is a city-owned utility, it can borrow money cheaply and pass those savings to customers. Commercial banks charge 7–9% for similar loans — CPP cuts that nearly in half.
30%

Federal Tax Credit — A Huge Deal

The U.S. government gives back 30% of the cost of clean energy installations as a tax credit. On a $10 million generator project, that's $3 million back in the data center's pocket — making island power one of the most cost-effective energy options available anywhere.
