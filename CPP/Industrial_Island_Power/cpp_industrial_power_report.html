I.

The Machine: GE Vernova 7HA.03

The 7HA.03 is the highest-output 60 Hz gas turbine in GE Vernova's HA fleet — purpose-built for applications where capacity, ramp speed, and fuel flexibility must coexist.

The 7HA.03's fuel flexibility encompasses shale gas, high-ethane blends, hydrogen blends, #2 diesel, and crude oils — critical for a municipal utility where contractual fuel diversification is a board-level risk management requirement. The world's first commercial 7HA.03 plant, FPL's Dania Beach Clean Energy Center, entered commercial operation on June 1, 2022 — providing a fully validated operational reference point for regulatory and financing purposes.

A 300 MW hyperscale data center operating on simple cycle alone leaves 130 MW of dispatchable surplus — enough to serve CPP's existing industrial customer base or generate net PJM wholesale revenue exceeding $30–50M annually at current capacity pricing.

For CPP's specific use case, simple cycle is the recommended initial configuration: faster deployment (18–24 months vs. 36+ for CCGT), smaller physical footprint for urban industrial sites, and elimination of HRSG, steam turbine, and cooling tower complexity. The combined cycle pathway remains available as a Phase 2 expansion if the data center scales beyond 300 MW or CPP pursues PJM merchant market participation at scale.

CPP currently supplements its three 138 kV purchased power interconnections with three combustion turbine generating units and ten diesel generators distributed across its network — providing limited peaking capacity but no meaningful baseload generation. The utility is structurally a price-taker: exposed to PJM wholesale market volatility on every megawatt it purchases.

A 300 MW hyperscale data center would effectively double CPP's current system load — making a substation upgrade alone insufficient without a co-located generation asset. The 7HA.03 resolves this gap exactly. CPP's Optional Large Industrial rate tier already anticipates customers with 10 MW+ demand served at 138,000 volts primary — a 300 MW customer is 30× that threshold, requiring a purpose-built generation-to-load interconnect structure.

CPP's three existing 138 kV interconnections provide a significant head start on PJM interconnection study requirements. The new generation plant injects at 138 kV, aligning with the existing transmission loop rather than requiring new transmission infrastructure across Cuyahoga County.

III.

Upgrade Architecture: Four Integrated Layers

The substation upgrade is a four-layer integrated system — not a single capital project. Each layer builds on the last, and each creates independent value.

Layer 01 · Transmission

Dedicated Interconnection Substation

A 300 MW load requires 138 kV primary delivery — aligned with CPP's existing transmission backbone. The substation upgrade includes step-up transformers from turbine terminals (~18–27 kV) to 138 kV, Gas Insulated Switchgear (GIS) for urban footprint constraints, a dedicated 138/13.8 kV step-down bank for campus distribution, and full SCADA integration into CPP's control architecture.

Infrastructure investment: $40–80M. Substation footprint: 3–5 acres urban GIS configuration.
Layer 02 · Generation

7HA.03 Plant — Simple Cycle Configuration

Simple cycle is optimal for CPP's initial deployment: 430 MW output covers 300 MW data center load with 130 MW available for system sales, faster deployment timeline (18–24 months), smaller site footprint, and elimination of HRSG/steam turbine/cooling tower complexity. Combined cycle upgrade remains available as Phase 2 if the campus scales to 500–600 MW or PJM merchant participation intensifies.

Plant capital cost: ~$500–650M turnkey at $1,200–1,500/kW. Site requirement: 10–15 acres minimum.
Layer 03 · Markets

PJM Interconnection & Capacity Markets

CPP's Industrial Power Division participates in PJM as a registered generator — unlocking energy market revenue from 130 MW surplus capacity dispatched into day-ahead and real-time markets. The 7HA.03's 75 MW/min ramp rate qualifies the plant for PJM regulation and spinning reserve ancillary services. The Reliability Pricing Model (RPM) capacity auction provides base revenue regardless of energy dispatch, de-risking the plant's operating economics.

Estimated PJM revenue from 130 MW surplus: $30–50M annually at current RPM + energy pricing.
Layer 04 · Decarbonization

Hydrogen Readiness & Fuel Pathway

The DLN2.6e combustion system demonstrates sub-2.5 ppm NOx at 15%–100% load — the performance data base needed for OEPA Title V permitting in Cuyahoga County's nonattainment air quality zone. The 50% hydrogen co-fire capability aligns with green hydrogen production at the Port of Cleveland and any future PEM electrolysis facility, providing a credible carbon reduction trajectory without stranding the asset.

A 50% H₂ blend at full load reduces carbon intensity by approximately 40% vs. natural gas-only operation.
IV.

The Industrial Power Division Structure

CPP's existing power supply contracts need not be touched. The Industrial Power Division operates as a fully parallel enterprise — same municipal parent, separate financial architecture.

CPP currently purchases most of its power through short and long-term contracts with regional utilities and power suppliers via its three 138 kV interconnections, making it structurally a price-taker on wholesale power with no owned generation hedge beyond small peaking units. The Industrial Power Division does not replace or modify these contracts — it creates a parallel generation pathway with no dependency on the wholesale market for the data center load.

Division Element Structure Key Provision
Enterprise Fund Separate sub-fund by City Council ordinance, ring-fenced from CPP general fund Residential ratepayers bear zero financial exposure from industrial division capital
Rate Schedule Negotiated directly with data center under 15–20 year Power Purchase Agreement Fixed rate $55–70/MWh all-in; minimum demand charge covers debt service regardless of consumption
Debt Instrument Revenue bonds backed exclusively by PPA cash flow Bond coverage ratio 1.3–1.5× at assumed PPA pricing; no General Obligation backing required
PJM Market Access Register plant as PJM Capacity Resource; sell surplus 130 MW into energy + ancillary markets Energy + capacity + regulation revenue estimated $30–50M/yr at current pricing
Purchased Power Bypass Data center load served entirely from owned generation — no wholesale market exposure As existing contracts expire, CPP negotiates smaller replacements or eliminates them for industrial segment

The Power Purchase Agreement with the data center operator is the linchpin: a 20-year off-take at $55–70/MWh makes the revenue bond issuance viable, caps CPP's generation cost well below PJM merchant pricing, and provides the data center with rate certainty unavailable from any investor-owned utility. It is a mutual lock-in that benefits both parties.

When existing wholesale contracts expire, CPP retains the option to negotiate smaller replacement contracts — or none at all for the industrial segment — having replaced that portion of its power supply with owned generation. Over time, the Industrial Power Division's PJM revenues can be structured to cross-subsidize lower rates for CPP's residential customers, creating a durable political constituency for the program within Cleveland City Council.

V.

Legal Framework & Constitutional Authority

Three overlapping legal foundations give CPP exceptional flexibility unavailable to investor-owned utilities operating under PUCO jurisdiction.

Ohio Constitution · Art. XVIII §4

Municipal Home Rule — Generation Authority

Any municipality may acquire, construct, own, lease and operate within or without its corporate limits any public utility the product or service of which is to be supplied to the municipality or its inhabitants. This is CPP's core generation authority — no PUCO approval required, governed solely by City Council ordinance.

Ohio Constitution · Art. XVIII §6

Surplus Sale Authority — 50% Rule

Municipalities owning a public utility may sell surplus product or service to those outside the municipality in an amount not exceeding fifty percent of the total service or product supplied within the municipality. The Ohio Supreme Court (CEI v. Cleveland, 2021) confirmed this right — CPP's 130 MW surplus from the 7HA.03 falls well within this ceiling.

ORC §4928.73 · Enacted 2025

Mercantile Customer Self-Power System

Creates a direct-wire generation structure for commercial or industrial customers consuming more than 700,000 kWh/year — connecting behind the customer's utility meter without using any public utility distribution infrastructure. A 300 MW data center at ~2.6 billion kWh/year qualifies overwhelmingly, enabling CPP to bypass its own distribution system for the data center feed.

CEI v. Cleveland · 2021

Purchased Power vs. Generated Power Distinction

The Ohio Supreme Court held that Article XVIII §4 authorizes purchase of electricity only for supplying it to the municipality or inhabitants — not for external resale. However, generated power is not subject to this constraint. CPP can sell all generated output to in-city industrial customers without restriction. The critical structuring implication: own the turbine, don't buy and resell.

PUCO Exemption

No State Rate Regulation

CPP, as a municipal electric utility, is explicitly excluded from PUCO's definition of "electric utility" under ORC §4928.01(11). Cleveland City Council is the sole governing body for CPP's rates, capital investments, and organizational structure. A new industrial rate schedule, PPA, and enterprise fund sub-division require only Council ordinance — not a multi-year rate case.

ORC §715.02

Inter-Municipal Cooperation Authority

Two or more municipal corporations may enter into agreement for joint construction or management of any public work or utility benefiting each municipality. This enables CPP's Industrial Power Division to eventually serve neighboring inner-ring suburbs (Garfield Heights, Maple Heights, Brooklyn) under negotiated agreements — expanding the division's revenue base without violating territorial constraints.

The CEI v. Cleveland decision created a precise structuring requirement: CPP must own and operate the 7HA.03 as a generation asset — not purchase and resell wholesale power — to avoid the Article XVIII §4 resale prohibition. The Industrial Power Division must be structured as a generator, not a broker. The turbine ownership model satisfies this requirement completely.

VII.

Strategic Outcomes

The Industrial Power Division is not an incremental improvement to CPP's operating model. It is a structural transformation in what CPP is.

Today, CPP obtains most of its power through short and long-term contracts with regional utilities and power suppliers — making it a net buyer on the wholesale market with no owned generation hedge. The 7HA.03 paired to a 300 MW hyperscale data center anchor tenant reverses that equation entirely: CPP becomes a price-setter for its largest customers, a net seller in PJM markets during surplus hours, and a generation utility — not merely a distribution utility — for the first time in its modern history.

Four Structural Transformations

CPP becomes a price-setter, not a price-taker — owned generation at ~$35–45/MWh marginal cost vs. purchased power at $60–90/MWh in peak periods
The data center provides a guaranteed 20-year off-take — eliminating merchant generation risk and enabling investment-grade revenue bond financing
130 MW surplus sells into PJM energy, capacity, and ancillary markets — generating net revenue that can cross-subsidize residential rate stability
CPP's system capacity effectively doubles — positioning Cleveland to attract additional large industrial and technology tenants currently priced out of CPP's service territory due to capacity constraints
A hydrogen co-fire pathway provides a credible decarbonization trajectory, supporting CPP's "green city on a blue lake" positioning without stranding the capital asset
The legal structure established by the Industrial Power Division creates a replicable template for future large industrial customers — each anchored by a long-term PPA and financed independently of ratepayers
PUCO exemption means the entire program moves at City Council speed — ordinances, not rate cases. Execution timeline is political, not regulatory.
The division fulfills CPP's 1906 founding mandate — a municipally owned generator providing affordable, reliable power to Cleveland's industrial and commercial base, free from investor-owned utility pricing

The constitutional home rule authority under Article XVIII, the new ORC §4928.73 mercantile customer pathway, and CPP's PUCO-exempt status combine to make this one of the most legally clean and structurally elegant development frameworks available to any Ohio municipality. The binding constraint is not legal or technical — it is whether Cleveland's political leadership has the appetite to execute, and whether the data center developer will sign a 20-year off-take agreement that makes the bond issuance viable.
