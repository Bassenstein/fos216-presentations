Executive Summary

The most ambitious
lakefront proposal
in American history

Burke Lakefront Airport occupies 450 acres of Lake Erie shoreline — a site debated for redevelopment for over two decades — and almost none of it is open to the public. Every proposal to date has treated Burke as a consumption site: parks, hotels, condos. This proposal makes it a production site — one that transforms Lake Erie water into green hydrogen, green ammonia, clean electricity, and data center cooling, while layering civic space and a complete educational pipeline on top.

"Instead of asking Congress and the FAA to close an airport for a park, Cleveland asks for closure to build a nationally significant clean energy infrastructure project — a Hydrogen Hub anchor site, an offshore wind onshore hub, and a Great Lakes clean energy demonstration campus." — Water-to-Energy Campus Concept, 2024

The campus integrates six converging technology pillars: Lake Erie water purification and PEM electrolysis producing green hydrogen at industrial scale; Sierra Energy FastOx gasification converting Cleveland MSW into syngas; Hyliion KARNO linear generator microgrid providing island-capable on-site power; Haber-Bosch green ammonia synthesis; a 60 MW hyperscale data center cooled by Lake Erie deep water; and active discharge reoxygenation returning expelled water to full ecological health. At full operation, the campus generates approximately $302M/year in gross revenue while creating 1,200+ permanent high-quality jobs.

Campus Master Plan

Twelve zones.
One integrated
energy campus.

The 450-acre Burke site is organized into twelve purpose-defined zones. The two runway strips — 6,604 ft and 5,197 ft of reinforced concrete — provide ideal foundations for heavy industrial buildings. The site's landfill substrate, while requiring pile foundations for heavy loads, is notably clean by industrial redevelopment standards. City retains fee ownership via 99-year ground lease.

At 500 MW electrolyser input, water consumption is ~3.6M litres/day — trivial against Lake Erie's 25,700 km² surface. The intake station doubles as a Great Lakes Water Quality monitoring hub partnered with Cleveland Water Alliance and NOAA's Great Lakes Environmental Research Laboratory.

Zone 3 · Phase 2 · 2030–2032

Sierra Energy FastOx Gasification

FastOx shaft gasification — derived from Kaiser Steel blast furnace technology by Sierra Energy (Davis, CA) — converts Cleveland MSW at temperatures exceeding 2,200°C using oxygen and steam injection. No preprocessing required. Waste descends through drying, pyrolysis, gasification, and partial oxidation zones producing clean syngas (H₂ + CO) and inert vitrified slag suitable for road aggregate. The syngas feeds the Haber-Bosch ammonia synthesis loop after water-gas shift reaction and CO₂ removal. Cleveland pays gate fees to tip waste at the campus instead of landfill, converting a $15–20M/year cost center into a revenue stream. Zero liquid discharge design.

Hyliion KARNO Microgrid

25 containerized KARNO modules in 20-ft ISO containers (2 MW each = 50 MW total), occupying a pad of approximately 150×80 m. Haber-Bosch Green Ammonia Synthesis

1,200 t/day green ammonia plant combining electrolytic H₂ with nitrogen from an on-site Air Separation Unit. Synthesis loop operates at 150–300 bar, 400–500°C over iron catalyst with K₂O and Al₂O₃ promoters. Only 15–25% converts per pass; unreacted gas recycles. The Core Process

Lake Erie water
to green hydrogen
in six stages

1
Stage 1 · Intake

Lake Erie water draw

A submerged intake crib positioned 300–600 metres offshore draws water below the thermal stratification layer — consistently 38–55°F year-round regardless of surface ice or summer warming. Fish exclusion screens at mesh 1 mm, approach velocity controlled to under 0.15 m/s, meet Great Lakes Water Quality Agreement (GLWQA) requirements non-negotiably. Raw intake capacity: 40,000 gallons per minute feeding the shoreline pump station. At 500 MW electrolyser load, water consumption is approximately 3.6 million litres per day — a trivial draw on a lake with 25,700 km² of surface area.

2
Stage 2 · Water Treatment

Purification to electrolysis grade

The treatment train achieves electrolysis-grade feedwater in three stages: (1) Softening via ion exchange removes calcium, magnesium, and other hardness ions that would foul PEM membranes; (2) Reverse osmosis reduces TDS by 95%+ and removes particulates; (3) Final deionisation (DI) polishing achieves <1 µS/cm conductivity — the specification required by PEM electrolyser manufacturers to protect the proton exchange membrane and avoid accelerated stack degradation. This ultra-pure water is what makes the electrolyser farm's claimed 80,000-hour stack life achievable.

3
Stage 3 · Electrolysis

Splitting water with electricity

500 MW of PEM electrolyser current passes through the purified water: 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂. The proton exchange membrane allows only protons (H⁺) to pass, producing high-purity hydrogen at the cathode. At 50 kWh/kg system efficiency, 500 MW produces approximately 10,000 kg of hydrogen per hour — 180 tonnes per day. PEM output H₂ is approximately 99.9% pure, at 30–50 bar, containing trace moisture. Stack lifetime at rated current density: approximately 80,000 hours (about 9 years) before major stack replacement, a primary O&M cost driver.

4
Stage 4 · Purification & Drying

H₂ conditioning for use and storage

PEM output hydrogen contains trace moisture and <0.1% oxygen. A pressure swing adsorption (PSA) drier or membrane drier reduces dewpoint below −40°C, protecting high-pressure storage vessels from internal corrosion. A catalytic deoxo unit (palladium catalyst) removes O₂ to <1 ppm where required by storage specifications. For direct-to-KARNO supply, the deoxo step is often omitted. Final purity: 99.95%+ H₂, dry, at 30–50 bar — ready for buffer storage, KARNO fuel rail, or compression to 350 bar for tube storage and transport.

5
Stage 5 · Buffer Storage

Operational decoupling and flexibility

Even a small buffer vessel (50–200 kg H₂ capacity) provides critical operational flexibility: absorbs electrolyser output during KARNO low-load periods; supplies the KARNO during the ~5-minute electrolyser startup or brief outage; decouples electrolyser operating schedule from generator demand schedule; enables KARNO "hot standby" mode. The KARNO can start in approximately 30 seconds and reach full power in four minutes. A buffer vessel sized for 30–60 minutes of full-load KARNO operation is sufficient for smooth operations. The 72-hour bulk storage field (Zone 9) provides the strategic buffer for grid services and demand response.

6
Stage 6 · Power Generation

The KARNO: hydrogen to electricity

Hydrogen enters the KARNO's external thermal reactor where it undergoes catalytic flameless oxidation — reacting with air at 400–800°C rather than the 1,500°C+ of a combustion flame. This produces heat with essentially zero NOx (no high-temperature flame front) and zero CO₂ (no carbon in the fuel). Heat transfers to the sealed helium working fluid. Helium expands, driving four synchronized linear pistons through magnetic fields to generate electricity — similar to a free-piston Stirling cycle. The cold side rejection loop at ~80°C hot water feeds the campus district heating network. Exhaust: water vapour only.

The Burke campus creates a five-tier educational pipeline connecting every level of Cleveland's academic ecosystem to real careers in clean energy — and uniquely, every tier uses the Burke campus itself as a live classroom. No simulation. No approximation. Real industrial hydrogen infrastructure at real operating scale.

Cleveland Public Schools

Early Awareness & Environmental Literacy

Field trips from Cleveland Metropolitan School District (CMSD) elementary schools to the Energy Discovery Centre — the glass-floored observation deck above the electrolyser farm. Students in grades 3–6 watch hydrogen being made from lake water in real time, then trace it through the KARNO generator to power the lights in the building they're standing in. Curriculum modules co-developed with CMSD science teachers aligned to Ohio science standards.

Tier 2 · High School
CMSD · MC² STEM · John Hay

Two Pathways: Technician Track & Engineering Track

MC² STEM High School, John Hay STEM, and Early College High School are natural feeders. The campus creates two distinct pathways:

Technician Pathway: Junior technician apprenticeships for 10th–12th graders — 10–15 paid hours per week on-site. Students rotate through water treatment operations, electrolyser monitoring, KARNO module checks, and control room data logging. These are entry-level positions with union-negotiated wage scale, structured mentorship from licensed plant operators, and a guaranteed Tri-C interview upon graduation. Burke becomes one of the SWAG program's anchor employers.

Engineering Pathway: AP Chemistry, Physics, and Environmental Science co-taught by CMSD teachers and CWRU faculty using Burke plant data as primary source material. A student's AP Chemistry lab assignment might be analysing the purity of hydrogen output from the electrolyser. Dual enrolment via Ohio's College Credit Plus earns Tri-C credit for courses taken on or near Burke.

Tier 3 · Community College
Cuyahoga Community College (Tri-C)

New Credentials for a New Industry

Tri-C's Workforce Innovation division — Metropolitan Campus 3 km from Burke — partners with the campus to create entirely new Ohio credentials that don't currently exist. The Burke campus creates demand for workers with skills no Ohio college currently certifies:
